Basic Facts About HIV/AIDS

Understanding the essentials of HIV and AIDS globally and locally.

Understanding HIV/AIDS

Since the beginning of the pandemic, 85.6 million people have acquired HIV, and more than 40 million have died of AIDS-related illnesses. This section provides key facts to help you understand HIV/AIDS, its transmission, and prevention.

What is HIV/AIDS?

HIV:
Human Immunodeficiency Virus is the virus that causes AIDS. It resides and multiplies in white blood cells, weakening the immune system. Learn More →
AIDS:
Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome is the advanced stage of HIV infection, where the immune system is severely damaged, leading to vulnerability to infections and illnesses like pneumonia or cancer.

Key Questions

How do I know if I’m infected?
Early symptoms may include mild flu-like signs or swollen glands, but many remain asymptomatic. Testing is the only way to confirm HIV status.
Can I tell if someone has HIV/AIDS?
No, an infected person can appear healthy; HIV/AIDS cannot be diagnosed by appearance alone.
How quickly does HIV progress to AIDS?
Progression varies—some develop AIDS soon after infection, while others remain symptom-free for over a decade. Early treatment with antiretroviral drugs (ART) can delay this significantly.
How many people are affected?
Globally, over 39 million people live with HIV. In the U.S., 1.2 million are affected, with 32,000 new diagnoses in 2021, and 13% unaware of their status.

Transmission of HIV

HIV is carried in blood, semen, vaginal secretions, and breast milk, and is transmitted through:

  • Unprotected vaginal or anal intercourse with an infected person.
  • Unprotected oral sex (lower risk but still possible).
  • Sharing needles or injection equipment with someone who has HIV.
  • Mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding (reduced to 1% or less with early treatment in the U.S.).

HIV is not transmitted through casual contact (e.g., hugging, kissing, sharing utensils), air, food, insects, or screened blood transfusions in the U.S.

HIV Testing and Prevention

How do I know if I’m infected?

Testing is critical as symptoms may not appear. Rapid tests (20-40 minutes) or home kits like OraQuick are available.

How can I reduce my risk?

Use condoms consistently, limit partners, avoid sharing needles, and consider PrEP if at high risk. PEP can prevent infection if taken within 72 hours of exposure.
